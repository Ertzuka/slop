The Finnish Criminal Code (Finnish: Rikoslaki, Swedish: Strafflagen) is the primary body of criminal law in Finland. It defines criminal offenses, establishes penalties, and outlines general principles of criminal responsibility. Originally enacted in 1889, it has been extensively reformed—especially during the late 20th century—to reflect modern legal standards and human rights norms under the Constitution of Finland and European law.

1. Core Principles

Finnish criminal law is built on several fundamental principles:

Legality (Nullum crimen sine lege) – No one can be punished unless the act was defined as a crime by law at the time it was committed.

Proportionality – Punishments must correspond to the seriousness of the crime.

Culpability – Criminal liability requires intent or negligence.

Human dignity and rights protection – Punishments must respect constitutional and international human rights standards.

Rehabilitation – The justice system emphasizes reintegration rather than purely punitive measures.

These principles are influenced by European legal traditions and international treaties such as the European Convention on Human Rights.

2. Structure of Finnish Criminal Law

Finnish criminal law is primarily contained in:

The Finnish Criminal Code

Various special criminal statutes

The Constitution of Finland

European Union and international obligations

The Criminal Code is divided into general provisions and chapters covering specific crimes.

3. General Part of the Criminal Code

The general provisions regulate how criminal responsibility is determined.

Criminal Liability

A person is criminally liable if they:

commit a criminal act intentionally or through negligence

are criminally responsible (generally aged 15 or older)

Intent and Negligence

Crimes may be committed:

Intentionally

Through negligence

Through gross negligence (more serious carelessness)

Attempt and Participation

The law also criminalizes:

Attempted crimes

Complicity (aiding or encouraging a crime)

Defenses

Certain circumstances remove criminal liability, including:

Self-defense

Necessity

Lack of criminal responsibility (e.g., severe mental illness)

4. Categories of Crimes

The Criminal Code contains numerous chapters covering specific offenses. Major categories include:

Crimes Against Life and Health

Examples:

Murder

Manslaughter

Assault

Aggravated assault

These crimes protect bodily integrity and life.

Sexual Offenses

Finnish law criminalizes acts such as:

Rape

Sexual assault

Sexual abuse of a minor

Sexual crime legislation has been strengthened significantly in recent years to emphasize consent-based definitions of rape.

Property Crimes

Crimes involving unlawful taking or damage to property include:

Theft

Aggravated theft

Fraud

Embezzlement

Robbery

Damage to property

Economic and Corporate Crimes

Finland has detailed laws covering:

Tax fraud

Accounting offenses

Insider trading

Money laundering

These crimes protect financial integrity and economic systems.

Public Order and State Crimes

Examples include:

Bribery and corruption

Official misconduct

Treason and espionage

These protect the functioning of government and society.

Drug Offenses

Drug crimes are defined in cooperation with international conventions and include:

Drug use

Drug trafficking

Aggravated drug offenses

5. Types of Punishments

Finnish criminal law emphasizes proportional and rehabilitative penalties.

Main sanctions include:

Fines

The most common punishment, calculated using a day-fine system based on the offender’s income.

Imprisonment

Two main types:

Fixed-term imprisonment (usually from 14 days to 12 years)

Life imprisonment for the most serious crimes (e.g., aggravated murder)

Life prisoners may later apply for release after a minimum period.

Conditional Imprisonment

Short prison sentences may be suspended if the offender complies with conditions.

Community Service

Offenders may perform community service instead of serving short prison sentences.

Juvenile Punishments

For offenders aged 15–17, courts may impose:

Juvenile punishment

supervision measures

rehabilitation programs

6. Criminal Procedure and Courts

Criminal cases are processed within the Finnish court system.

Key institutions include:

Finnish Police – investigate crimes

National Prosecution Authority – prosecutors decide whether to bring charges

District Courts of Finland – first-instance criminal trials

Courts of Appeal of Finland – appellate courts

Supreme Court of Finland – highest court for criminal cases

7. Human Rights and International Influence

Finnish criminal law operates within broader European legal frameworks.

Important influences include:

The European Union

The Council of Europe

The European Convention on Human Rights

These institutions affect legislation on issues such as fair trials, prisoners’ rights, and criminal procedure.

✅ In essence: Finnish criminal law defines crimes and punishments through the Finnish Criminal Code, emphasizing legality, proportionality, and rehabilitation while operating within a constitutional and European human-rights framework.
